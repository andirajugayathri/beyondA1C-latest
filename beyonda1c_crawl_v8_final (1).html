Three discoveries. One screening program.

For years, the team at the Boston Diabetes Research Institute has been asking a hard question: why do so many patients develop devastating complications even when their diabetes appears well controlled? That work led to three connected discoveries that form the scientific foundation of BeyondA1C.

Finding 01

A1C has a blind spot

A1C reflects average glucose but says nothing about the oxidative stress, nerve injury, or vascular damage that may already be underway in a patient's tissues.

Finding 02

Neuropathy is the sentinel

BDRI's research establishes that nerve dysfunction tends to appear first. Peripheral neuropathy is an independent predictor of microvascular complications, including retinopathy, nephropathy, and cardiovascular autonomic neuropathy, in diabetic patients (Vinik AI et al. Endocrinol Metab Clin North Am. 2004;33(2):405–415). BeyondA1C uses DPNCheck® and BDRI's algorithm to generate a composite risk profile across these complication domains.

BDRI's first key finding was documenting the specific ways A1C fails as a standalone measure of diabetic complication risk.

Limitation 01

A1C misses silent, early damage

Up to 50% of patients with diabetic neuropathy are completely asymptomatic in the early and moderate stages. Nerve damage accumulates for years with no signal in a standard blood panel. By the time symptoms appear, the window for meaningful intervention has often closed.

↗ Damage begins years before any clinical sign
Limitation 02

Good A1C does not mean no complications

Glycemic control alone does not fully capture risk for diabetic foot complications. Even patients with A1C levels considered well-controlled remain vulnerable to ulceration and amputation due to factors such as neuropathy, microvascular disease, and cumulative glycemic exposure. (Journal of Diabetes and its Complications, 2020;34(10):107638.)

Limitation 03

A1C does not screen for microvascular damage

A1C reflects glycated hemoglobin. It does not measure nerve conduction, retinal vascular integrity, or kidney filtration. Those are separate biological processes that need direct measurement. Relying on A1C alone leaves the most important window for early intervention completely dark.

Peripheral neuropathy as a sentinel marker. Screened at the point of care.

BDRI's second key finding was that diabetic peripheral neuropathy is not just one complication among many. It is a sentinel marker: the first sign of a broader microvascular process that, left undetected, leads to retinopathy, nephropathy, and cardiovascular autonomic neuropathy (Vinik AI et al. Endocrinol Metab Clin North Am. 2004). BeyondA1C was built on that insight. No specialist training needed. Results in under one minute.

Vision-Threatening Retinopathy

Peripheral neuropathy is associated with both the presence and increasing severity of diabetic retinopathy, reflecting shared microvascular pathology rather than a singular causal pathway (Feldman EL et al. Lancet Diabetes Endocrinol. 2019;7(12):938–948. DOI: 10.1016/S2213-8587(19)30059-6). Population studies demonstrate that retinopathy is significantly more common in patients with peripheral neuropathy, with several-fold higher prevalence compared to those without neuropathy (Ponirakis G et al. BMJ Open Diabetes Res Care. 2020;8(1):e001014. DOI: 10.1136/bmjdrc-2019-001014; Raman R et al. SAGE Open Med. 2022;10:20503121221099946. DOI: 10.1177/20503121221099946). Cross-sectional and mechanistic data show that neuropathy severity tracks with retinopathy severity, with markedly higher neuropathy prevalence in advanced retinopathy stages (Tesfaye S et al. Diabetes Care. 2010;33(10):2285–2293. DOI: 10.2337/dc10-1303). Emerging mechanistic and imaging data confirm that neural injury and retinal microvascular damage are biologically linked, sharing common pathways including microvascular ischemia and neurodegeneration (Feldman EL et al. 2019). This association reflects BDRI research findings and does not constitute a cleared indication for retinopathy screening.

🫘

Diabetic Nephropathy

Decline in kidney function in diabetes is not uniformly accompanied by albuminuria. Contemporary guidelines and epidemiologic studies recognize a non-albuminuric phenotype of diabetic kidney disease, in which significant eGFR loss occurs despite normal or minimally elevated albumin excretion. BeyondA1C-measured peripheral neuropathy provides an additional marker of systemic microvascular injury that may help identify patients at risk for kidney function decline, including those who would not be flagged by albuminuria alone. This association reflects BDRI research findings and does not constitute a cleared indication for nephropathy screening.

Sources: KDIGO 2022 Clinical Practice Guideline for Diabetes Management in CKD. Kidney Int. 2022;102(5S):S1–S127; Selvarajah S et al. JAMA Netw Open. 2020;3(10):e2025497

What is BeyondA1C and why would my doctor order it?
BeyondA1C uses a small handheld device to measure how well the sural nerve in your ankle conducts electrical signals. Because peripheral neuropathy is one of the first signs of microvascular disease, this short test gives your doctor a useful window into your overall complication risk, including neuropathy, kidney disease, and eye disease.
Is the test painful or time-consuming?
Not at all. The full test takes under one minute. You might feel a brief, mild tingling, a bit like when a foot falls asleep. Most patients are surprised by how quick it is, and there is nothing you need to do beforehand.
Can I have complications if my A1C is in a good range?
Yes, and that is exactly why BeyondA1C was developed. Glycemic control alone does not fully capture risk for diabetic foot complications. Even patients with A1C levels below 7% remain vulnerable to ulceration and amputation due to factors such as neuropathy, microvascular disease, and cumulative glycemic exposure. (Journal of Diabetes and its Complications, Volume 34, Issue 10, October 2020, 107638.) Managing blood glucose matters a great deal, but A1C cannot tell your doctor whether your nerves, kidneys, or eyes are already being affected. BeyondA1C addresses that gap.
